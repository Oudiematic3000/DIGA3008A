Reading - How a Screen Reader User Accesses the Internet

This article (video mostly) covers how screen reader applications interpret web pages and the accessibility challenges that face visually impaired users. While completely coincidental, the tree structure layout of HTML based webpages makes them extremely accessible. Screen Reader Applications (SRAs here forward) can access the hidden formatting of HTML webpages to convey information like the website title, headings, navigations, hypertext, sections, etc. This is extremely cool but, I wonder how much more difficult it will become for SRAs to interpret websites as UX/UI design trends become more creative and dynamic. Will we see a greater push for intentionally designed accessibility features on websites, or will SRAs be improved to deal with nonstandard layouts? I'm quite interested in the struggle a lot of digital media deals with between maintaining the intended experience of it while also making it as accessible as possible. In the article, an example is shown for a website that uses scripts to visually hide certain headings on the web page until various fields are filled in. The user's SRA however, reads the headings as occurring just after the title, even though they are not visually apparent on screen. Additionally, multiple input fields lack proper descriptors and are skipped over. The website is arguably well designed and clear for able users, but borderline unusable for a visually impaired user. It is interesting to see how historically, the internet might have been more accessible in its simpler, earlier state. If there are any conventions or best-practices for accessibility in Javascript for WebDev, it would be very cool to learn them this semester.

Hypertext

Where do we go from here? Ariel Oudmayer 24 February 2025

Reading

Moulthrop's essay was a fulfilling read that brings up many poignant ideas about hypertext and the internet that are observable in our modern internet today. Let's rewind. We have a new technology. We can now link information to other, related information in a manner that can only be achieved through the digital access of this decentralized knowledge. It's called Hypertext. Go wild - The world is yours. Surely this would bring about massive cultural and educational upheaval in the world, but that's not exactly what we see. Hypertext, in theory, should democratize knowledge and the ability to educate yourself and others through the web. However, the essay compares our finding of this new system to May 68: 'we have taken over the station only to resume normal methods of broadcasting.' This new web is adapted to much like every other form of media has been in the past. It is snatched up by corporations and tech companies to advertise and modernise. Moulthrop emphasizes the apparent strangeness in the lack of sudden, radical change in culture with the advent of this new technology. Moulthrop posits a few explanations for this: Maybe it's because the enterprising generation at the time were always obsessed with the 'next big thing'; maybe it's because a 'revolution' looks fundamentally different in modern times. Observing the world today (as in 2025), can we say that the revolutionary hypertext culture is the status quo? The essay aptly states that 'Hypertext is all about connection, linkage, and affiliation.' The nature of hypertext is to mimic the way humans actually process knowledge and think thoughts. I don't think it's implausible to say that people didn't immediately epiphanise at the commercialising of hypertext because the concept is actually quite natural to us. The essay also asks if we'd want a revolution at all. What would happen if we pushed this system to its absolute limits? It warns about how systems taken to their extremes often do exactly the opposite of their intention. Looking at the world today, have we come to a point where a system designed to democratise knowledge gathering and sharing is instead being used to make knowledge exclusive or untrustworthy? 'Responsibility for the evolution of hypertext systems as genuine alternatives to the present information economy rests as much with software developers, social scientists, and literary theorists as it does with legislators and capitalists.' Well said, Moulthrop, and decades after the publishing of this essay- we still lack proper legislation around dissemination of information online. An irony of the modern web is that it takes the same amount of effort to be fully traceable as it does to be fully untraceable. As Humans do, we will continue to pioneer new technology before we've had the time to regulate last year's bleeding edge. With LLMs and GAI rapidly gaining steam, is there time for ethics and law to catch up before too much harm is done?
