    Ariel Oudmayer

    GitHub:

    It would be presumptuous of me to say I'm good friends with Git- but I'd at least like to say we're well acquainted. I have used it in many personal projects and have found a structure that works well and I've used it on occasion to borrow other people's solutions to extremely niche software problems. I'm excited to learn more of what it's capable of this year.

    Reading - How a Screen Reader User Accesses the Internet

    This article (video mostly) covers how screen reader applications interpret web pages and the accessibility challenges that face visually impaired users. While completely coincidental, the tree structure layout of HTML based webpages makes them extremely accessible. Screen Reader Applications (SRAs here forward) can access the hidden formatting of HTML webpages to convey information like the website title, headings, navigations, hypertext, sections, etc. This is extremely cool but, I wonder how much more difficult it will become for SRAs to interpret websites as UX/UI design trends become more creative and dynamic. Will we see a greater push for intentionally designed accessibility features on websites, or will SRAs be improved to deal with nonstandard layouts? I'm quite interested in the struggle a lot of digital media deals with between maintaining the intended experience of it while also making it as accessible as possible. In the article, an example is shown for a website that uses scripts to visually hide certain headings on the web page until various fields are filled in. The user's SRA however, reads the headings as occurring just after the title, even though they are not visually apparent on screen. Additionally, multiple input fields lack proper descriptors and are skipped over. The website is arguably well designed and clear for able users, but borderline unusable for a visually impaired user. It is interesting to see how historically, the internet might have been more accessible in its simpler, earlier state. If there are any conventions or best-practices for accessibility in Javascript for WebDev, it would be very cool to learn them this semester.

    Course Goals

    I would love to be able to use whatever website that comes out of this course as a personal CV. Javascript might be the most universally loved and hated language simultaneously and I would very much like to call myself competent in it.

    Hypertext

    Where do we go from here?

    Blog 2

    Ariel Oudmayer

    24 February 2025

    Reading

    Moulthrop's essay was a fulfilling read that brings up many poignant ideas about hypertext and the internet that are observable in our modern internet today. Let's rewind. We have a new technology. We can now link information to other, related information in a manner that can only be achieved through the digital access of this decentralized knowledge. It's called Hypertext. Go wild - The world is yours. Surely this would bring about massive cultural and educational upheaval in the world, but that's not exactly what we see. Hypertext, in theory, should democratize knowledge and the ability to educate yourself and others through the web. However, the essay compares our finding of this new system to May 68: 'we have taken over the station only to resume normal methods of broadcasting.'

    This new web is adapted to much like every other form of media has been in the past. It is snatched up by corporations and tech companies to advertise and modernise. Moulthrop emphasizes the apparent strangeness in the lack of sudden, radical change in culture with the advent of this new technology. Moulthrop posits a few explanations for this: Maybe it's because the enterprising generation at the time were always obsessed with the 'next big thing'; maybe it's because a 'revolution' looks fundamentally different in modern times. Observing the world today (as in 2025), can we say that the revolutionary hypertext culture is the status quo? The essay aptly states that 'Hypertext is all about connection, linkage, and affiliation.'

    The nature of hypertext is to mimic the way humans actually process knowledge and think thoughts. I don't think it's implausible to say that people didn't immediately epiphanise at the commercialising of hypertext because the concept is actually quite natural to us. The essay also asks if we'd want a revolution at all. What would happen if we pushed this system to its absolute limits? It warns about how systems taken to their extremes often do exactly the opposite of their intention. Looking at the world today, have we come to a point where a system designed to democratise knowledge gathering and sharing is instead being used to make knowledge exclusive or untrustworthy?

    'Responsibility for the evolution of hypertext systems as genuine alternatives to the present information economy rests as much with software developers, social scientists, and literary theorists as it does with legislators and capitalists.'

    Well said, Moulthrop, and decades after the publishing of this essay- we still lack proper legislation around dissemination of information online. An irony of the modern web is that it takes the same amount of effort to be fully traceable as it does to be fully untraceable. As Humans do, we will continue to pioneer new technology before we've had the time to regulate last year's bleeding edge. With LLMs and GAI rapidly gaining steam, is there time for ethics and law to catch up before too much harm is done?

    • Each release is sorted into half-year headings.

    • All entries older than April 2020 are missing due to a display error.

    Perhaps the only kudos to give to the Department of Basic Education website is that it is constantly updated and all links and information are current and relevant.

    South African Social Security Agency

    Homepage

    • The header gives no information about the department or website, but is rather a scrolling display of outdated slides (see COVID SRD application status notice)
    • Directly below the header we have a subtitle with the department motto that continuously scrolls across the page. Very distracting and still no information about what site I'm on.
    • The search bar does not in fact search for anything. It just reloads the page you're on when you press submit.
    • The 'SASSA Slide Gallery' is an animated cube, each side of which is an image and it turns every few seconds. Confusing style choice and completely inconsistent with the rest of the site.
    • There is a link to the PAIA Manual from 2021. There is a 2024 PAIA manual so this is just an out of date link.
    • On the right sidebar we have a broken Twitter display and a badly fitted Facebook display.

    This disclaimer is present on the contact page. The department responsible for social security and information protection warning that their site might be erroneous and they don't accept any responsibility for their misinformation or out of date information. The irony is palpable.

    The Internet as a Global Standard

    Can everybody keep up?

    Blog 8

    Ariel Oudmayer

    21 April 2025

    The use of the internet as a channel for communication, education, work and entertainment has become ubiquitous in the 21st century. If there was any silver lining of the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic it was the effect it had on global digital literacy and how much it showed us could be done using proper ICT tools. A study by David Mhlanga and Tankiso Moloi found that 4IR (fourth industrial revolution) tools had been implemented from primary school to tertiary education levels in South Africa to excellent success. The study notes the potential these tools have to decrease the strain on access to tertiary education which is a historical challenge in South Africa.

    Despite the obvious benefits this technology brings to the education and work sector, an estimated 41% of the global population still did not have access to the internet according to an article by Claire Daniolou in 2020. Daniolou notes the digital divide that occurs between many rural communities and the urban world. These rural areas are often of lower income, education level and infrastructure. This leads to a lack of access to broadband connection. The tragedy of this is the amount of culture, local specialisation and unique services and products that these communities could bring to the global platform but is lost to their circumstances.

    The nature of hypertext is to mimic the way humans actually process knowledge and think thoughts. I don't think it's implausible to say that people didn't immediately epiphanise at the commercialising of hypertext because the concept is actually quite natural to us. The essay also asks if we'd want a revolution at all. What would happen if we pushed this system to its absolute limits? It warns about how systems taken to their extremes often do exactly the opposite of their intention. Looking at the world today, have we come to a point where a system designed to democratise knowledge gathering and sharing is instead being used to make knowledge exclusive or untrustworthy?

    What can we do about it?

    Daniolou mentions that government agencies as well as non-government and academic organisations such as GoGlobal are making an effort to provide broadband access to rural areas and integrate them into the global channel for communication, e-commerce and online education.

    A 2024 article by Sarah Payne details how the quality of education and number of educated youth in South African rural areas has increased dramatically since the introduction of internet connectivity to said areas.

    Having had an extremely privileged and tech-assisted career through school, I feel the importance of using modern technology to aid education is absolutely crucial. Since the advent of the internet, it has become the cornerstone of every sector of human productivity. Access to the internet has morphed from a privilege into a human right in the modern world. We cannot allow people to be left behind from the fast-evolving landscape of digital education and communication.

    An Open-Reading of: Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results

    How much of our image do we control?

    Blog 9

    Ariel Oudmayer

    28 April 2025

    By Andrea Ballatore, Mark Graham and Shilad Sen

    'The cities and towns that we live in were once constructed from bricks, concrete, glass, and steel. Now, though, the places we inhabit also consist of digital augmentations (Graham 2013): digital code and digital content like data from Wikipedia, photographs from Flickr, restaurant reviews from Yelp, and algorithms controlled by Google that make information visible or invisible. The digital augmentations of places, in other words, are beginning to matter as much as their material counterparts…'

    In the modern world, our first impressions of places is their representations on the internet. In this modern world, however, there still exists many communities that do not have the infrastructure to support widespread internet access or large scale online contribution. The major consequences of this are information incoming to these communities being mostly non-local; and the digital representation of these communities and locations being controlled by foreign impressions of them. This creates a global disconnect between the reality of a place and the digital representation of that place which is largely created by foreign biases towards it. China, for example, has its own local search engine and sphere of social media. The result of this is the global image of china being largely built by US sources. A study done at Cornell University analysed the effect of 267 907 China-related articles published by The New York Times on population bias toward china and found that they explained 54% of the negative skewed variance in the public towards China. [1]

    'Although they portray themselves as neutral aggregators of information, search engines have created an informational infrastructure with precise characteristics, logics, and biases…The personalization of results is another researched aspect of search engines. Based on sophisticated and rich personal profiles, Google Search produces different content for different users, increasing and decreasing the visibility of links to deliver more relevant results. This process, according to Pariser (2011), might result in a “filter bubble,” in which users are systematically exposed only to content that matches their political and cultural inclinations…'

    Major Northern search engines such as Google, Yahoo and Bing all have massive influence on a user's perception of the world. They are particularly good at profiling users based on their tastes and opinions and providing content that is largely skewed towards the user's ideology. This creates a positive-feedback loop where users are only exposed to media to enforce their existing beliefs. Search engines are optimised to promote sensational content that invokes strong emotional response to boost engagement. This often includes extreme viewpoints that bolster a user's viewpoint or misinformation intending to radicalise them against opposition. [2]

    This figure shows the localness (the percentage of URLs on the search engine front page when searching the location's name that originate from said location) of each country's front page data. As seen in the figure, most countries have below 88 local pages displaying on their search engine front page. All countries with 88 or more URLs are countries with well-established Internet infrastructure such as the U.S., Canada, Russia and most of Europe. This illustrates how majority of the information and representation of countries in the Southern globe is controlled by Northern, more internet-adapted countries.

    'This investigation of the geography of Google Search results shows that wealthy and well-connected countries tend to have much more locally produced content that is visible about them than poor and poorly connected countries... This gives rise to a form of digital hegemony, whereby producers in a few countries get to define what is read by others. The United States, in particular, is a dominant content-producing force...'

    The digital divide between high-income communities with good internet infrastructure and low-income communities that lack these facilities continues to be a huge issue that is almost invisible globally. A large reason for the problem's invisibility is the underrepresentation of these disadvantaged places due to lack of locally produced content on them.

    The saying “When the U.S. sneezes, the world gets a cold,” aptly describes the amount of influence America has globally. The world adopts social norms and pop culture from the U.S. and is largely influenced by their politics and economy. In addition to this, it is clear from this study that the U.S. also controls our perception of other countries with majority of content about said countries being sourced from America. This control over the population's opinion of foreign cultures is extremely dangerous as it alienates all standards that deviate from those of the United States. Negative media portrayals of immigrants has been shown to increase hostility and prejudice towards foreigners in America [3]. This is likely to occur at a larger scale, throughout the West so long as majority of information about foreign countries is sourced from the U.S.

    'The presence of a strong publishing industry, using SciMago publication data as proxy, is the strongest predictor of the production of visible online content. The importance of the h-index in the model also shows that the impact of scientific publications is a better predictor of localness than the mere number of publications.'

    The study finds that the issue is not simply a matter of access to internet or local infrastructure. It finds that localness of online content is better predicted by the amount of scholarly content produced in the country. The problem then, is much deeper than one that can be solved by simply posting more local content. Perhaps the key to better online representation is the funding and bolstering of local educational and scientific facilities. This is not to say that there aren't steps to be taken by Google to minimise the bias created by their algorithm.

    'A few countries in the Global North play an inordinately large role in defining the digital augmentations of the Global South. Google's methods for ranking and representing are notoriously opaque…'

    If google were to make the source of webpages and their reason for ranking on the search page transparent to the public, it would largely decrease the media bias these sources would create by giving the user the awareness of how information they obtain may be skewed.

    References

    [1] J. Huang, G. Cook, and Y. Xie, “Large-scale quantitative evidence of media impact on public opinion toward China,” arXiv.org, https://arxiv.org/abs/2012.07575 (accessed Apr. 28, 2025).

    [2] L. Leffer, “How search engines boost misinformation,” Scientific American, https://www.scientificamerican.com/article/how-search-engines-boost-misinformation/ (accessed Apr. 28, 2025).

    [3] P. Conzo, G. Fuochi, L. Anfossi, F. Spaccatini, and C. O. Mosso, “Negative media portrayals of immigrants increase ingroup favoritism and hostile physiological and emotional reactions,” Scientific reports, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8361166/ (accessed Apr. 28, 2025).

    A Critical Reflection of: Social Capital and the Three Levels of Digital Divide

    The digital divide has changed.

    Blog 10

    Ariel Oudmayer

    5 May 2025

    By Massimo Ragnedda and Maria Laura Ruiu

    “The ongoing dispute is still between two opposite positions... on the one hand the Internet increases and improves social relationships; on the other it negatively affects face to face relationships. [...] This chapter proposes a nuanced perspective which investigates the potential new applications of social capital in the context of digital divide and explores how social and digital capitals are interrelated.”[1]

    While I agree that the internet has had a huge impact on social relationships, is it fair to say it negatively affects face to face relationships? There may be an argument that more social circles developing through digital platforms widens the digital divide as those unable to participate through internet access are isolated to groups on one side of the divide, but that doesn't imply any negative impact on face-to-face interactions where a digital platform is not involved. Personally, I've met and fostered relationships with many people online that has translated perfectly in a face-to-face dynamic.

    “Coleman defined social capital as 'a variety of entities having two characteristics in common: they all consist of some aspects of a social structure, and they facilitate certain actions of individuals who are within the structure.' [...] This makes social capital different than other forms of capitals. While economic capital is characterised by the possession of means of production, and human capital is based on individual skills, social capital is rooted in relationships between individuals.”[1]

    If Social Capital is gained through relationships and enable collectives to achieve certain goals as a result, how many extra opportunities are afforded to those with internet access and social media than those without? The lost social connections due to the digital divide is likely far more consequential than is often discussed.

    “Indeed, we do not access the Internet as a tabula rasa (blank slate), but on the background of our own social, cultural, political and personal capital. Our previous background influences how we access and use the Internet (first and second level) and how we reinvest valuable information in the social realm (third level) to improve our life chances.”[1]

    The severity of the digital divide has become more apparent as we've worked to lessen its impact. The first level saw the divide as a simple have vs have-not situation where the root of the problem was many individuals not having access to the internet. When this problem was solved in many communities, we found that the divide is still present in terms of how communities use the internet. Many still lacked the skills and resources to use the internet effectively to augment their livelihood. The third level now is the difference in social capital between users. A user's prior history and interaction with social relationships on the internet and its capabilities to form such connections greatly impacts the opportunities and engagement a user can accumulate online.

    “It seems that the “virtual communities” included in the individual “digital capital” of users, rely on an already existing social capital based on relationships and trust among people located in specific places. At the same time the Internet might become a connector for “social movements” that work in different contexts but share information and useful insights widening their social networks and promoting co-operation.” [1]

    I disagree that virtual communities rely on existing social capital. Virtual communities provide a source for digital and social capital by allowing for a public, openly accessible space for the sharing of ideas or information with no prior connections required. This is another reason the internet is a good connector for social movements. Many such movements are expanded due to a policy of online, anyone-is-welcome philosophy.

    “Social media platforms like Facebook allow users to maintain existing relationships while potentially expanding their networks through a 'snowball effect.' However, these platforms can both reinforce homogeneity and bonding social capital, or generate bridging capital—depending on how inclusive and diverse the networks become.”[1]

    This is a common case with social media platforms. Every social media platform has a target market and a political leaning. This results in many online spaces becoming echo chambers of same-minded people and thus the same ideas are validated repeatedly and become increasingly radical and extreme. Of what quality is any generated social capital from these spaces when its impact is reduced to the isolated space it originated from?

    “Cybertrust—confidence in the reliability of Internet platforms and the people on them—is essential to benefiting from online engagement. Those with greater digital capital are more able to navigate, protect their privacy, and maximize opportunities, while those with limited capital are more vulnerable to manipulation and narrowing algorithms.” [1]

    Having a wider sphere of engagement on the internet exposes the user to more people of differing opinions or backgrounds. It also creates a greater awareness in the user for misinformation or manipulation.

    References

    [1] Massimo Ragnedda and Maria Laura Ruiu (2017) Social capital and the three levels of digital divide. In Ragnedda M., Muschert G. eds. (2017), Theorizing Digital Divides, Routledge, pp. 21-34.

    An Ethical Internet

    A rant on the exploitations of the internet.

    Blog 12

    Ariel Oudmayer

    19 May 2025

    An internet that allows full access to all information for anyone. Accurate information that is directly sourced or cited. Representation of cultures with less access to the internet controlled by themselves. Freedom of expression of beliefs in a non-hateful manner for any user. Consistent and unbiased moderation of online social spaces.

    I believe that these are foundational for an ethical internet. Though I do think that the bigger issue preventing an ethical internet currently is the rampant display of ads for addictive vices. Ads for alcohol, porn and gambling are displayed haphazardly on webpages, before videos and are occasionally endorsed by creators or channels on platforms like YouTube or Twitch.

This is a dangerous and deceptive practice, the consequences of which are often severe financial, social or psychological damage. It also makes the internet a particularly risky place for recovering addicts to engage with.

    Europe government policy that abolishes any sort of advertisement of chance-based and digital currency exchange financial transactions in games is currently in the process of being legislated [1]. Ideally, any adds that promise the user the chance of winning any amount of money should be banned.

    Technology and art both have the power to cause massive social change. As a developer, I have a responsibility to uphold social justice and use my platform to amplify voices, represent others fairly and put the benefit of the user and the public above all.

    Approaching this workflow of ethics in my own work proves to be a complicated issue. As a beginner, my initial thought was ‘how can code be unethical at all?’ but since then I’ve come to learn about algorithmic biases, underrepresentation, assumptions about my user and discarding optimization (neglecting those with non-cutting-edge hardware).

    Even once you’re aware of these issues, it is still a challenge to create artefacts that minimise their presence. Creating a 100% ethical body of work may be an impossible task, but what’s important is to strive to reach that impossible ceiling.

    The challenge that the added requirement of optimization or accessibility options or proper representation may add is however, highly beneficial for learning better coding practices or design patterns.

    For my website, which relies heavily on an audio theme, I’ve learnt to consider users that may not have the capability to engage with the sound features- be that because of hardware or physical disability. This inspired the challenge to create a visualizer for my site that translates the audio features into a visual flair, thereby including more users.
